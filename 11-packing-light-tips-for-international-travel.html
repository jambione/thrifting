11 packing light tips for international travel

kara July 1, 2026

Let's just be clear - I'm not a pro at this. I did a ton of research to come up with a packing list for my trips to Italy in 2024 and Japan in 2026, and I also learned a LOT through experience. 

Since we always walk or take public transport when traveling internationally, lugging a massive suitcase is out of the question. We’re so committed to the backpack lifestyle that even on a trip to Arizona where we actually rented a car, we still didn’t bring traditional luggage. There's a unique freedom in skipping the airport check-in lines and completely eliminating the temptation to overpack.

Here are my packing light tips, international or not! (Note - I do not get paid for recommending any of the items mentioned here.)

1. Get a good backpacking pack. And by good, I mean not just durable and roomy, but that is comfortable to wear. A waist strap helps take pressure off your neck and shoulders. 

My backpack is the Osprey Sojourn Porter 65L. I love this pack. It fits everything, it's comfortable to wear, and the thing has about a million pockets. It is expandable, and it has a handle on the side for easy moving when it isn't on your back. I think the nicest feature, though, is that you can unclip the straps and unzip the back, and store all of the straps inside in case you do need to check it. I, however, have flown on 4 different flights with this backpack and was never forced to check it.

My husband has a Gregory Tetrad 75 which we like, but unfortunately it's been discontinued. The main benefit of it, other than being super roomy, is that it has a zip off day pack at the front. Osprey actually makes one that has similar features. We usually pack the Tetrad with everything we don't need on the flight and check it since it's our biggest bag. Then, we use our daypacks as carry-ons to hold the flight essentials, like laptops and charging cords.

I bought the kids both the Deuter Fox 40 for our trip to Italy, they were at the time 9 and 11. They used the same packs on our trip to Japan because of the Deuter Fox's adjustable 'Vari-Quick' strap system. It actually grows with the kids, meaning they can keep using these same bags all the way through middle and high school. My 13 year old is 5'6" right now, and it was perfectly comfortable for him. 

2. Compression bags are game changers. When we went to Italy, we invested in shrink pack travel bags. For the trip to Arizona, in addition to those we bought these Bagsmart compression packing cubes. These save space and keep your clothes organized.

3. Bring less than you think you need. There's no shortage of 'capsule wardrobe' guides online, but unless you're shooting a high-fashion editorial, you can pack way less than they suggest. Even knowing that, I still brought too much on our two-week trip to Japan. Here is everything I brought: 1 sleeveless linen blouse, 1 linen button down blouse, one linen dress, one linen skort, a pair of quick-dry hiking shorts, 1 pair of quick-dry hiking pants, 1 pair of linen pants, 3 t-shirts, a rain coat, 3 camisoles, 2 pajama shorts, 1 sweater, 1 scarf, 5 pairs of socks, and 5 pairs of underwear. I also brought 1 pair of sandals and 1 pair of sneakers. Once we got there, I basically moved into Uniqlo, picking up 3 camisoles, 3 t-shirts, and some quick-dry pants that actually fit. The moral of the story? If you're going to shop at your destination, leave the extra clothes at home. I should have done my homework on how amazing (and affordable) Japanese fashion is.

4. Buy what you need there. Skip packing the daily essentials unless you have specific skin sensitivities or allergies. Items like sunscreen, bug spray, and snacks are actually superior in Japan. As for over-the-counter medications like Sudafed, leave them behind—many countries have strict import rules for pharmaceuticals, so it’s safer to buy local equivalents. Plus, leaving the full-sized liquids at home ensures you won't have to check your backpack at the airport.

5. Bring the lightest water bottles possible. Don't over-pack water bottles. I brought four heavy ones to Italy to leverage Rome’s public fountains, but they quickly became dead weight. Switching to just two lightweight Nalgenes for sharing worked perfectly. Or, better yet: just buy a bottle there and recycle it before you leave.

7. Expandable travel bags are a must! On the trip to Italy, I only brought one, and it was not enough. We had to buy a suitcase. This trip, we brought two. We put all the souvenirs in the backpacks, and all of our dirty clothes in the expandable duffels. I bought this Osprey one recently, and although I don't like the strap, it is an extreme space saver.

8. Research the weather patterns. It sounds like a no-brainer, but I’ve had to buy an overpriced airport umbrella exactly three times because I failed to look ahead. Unless you’re headed to Japan—where brilliant $5 umbrellas are sold at every convenience store—pack a compact travel umbrella if rain is in the forecast.

Knowing we were heading into Japan’s rainy season, I waterproofed our rain jackets and carried my water-resistant, second-hand Tumi handbag, completely skipping any 'just in case' fleeces. Conversely, when we went to Arizona, I packed a hat and gloves for the chilly Grand Canyon winds, but left the 'just in case' raincoat at home. Pack for the reality of the climate, not the hypotheticals.

9. Leave your daily valuables at home. Not only do you not need your Lowe's card in Europe, but if you get pickpocketed (a lesson my mom learned the hard way in Athens), replacing a dozen cards is a logistical nightmare.

Go minimal with your keys, too. I valet park at the airport specifically so I can leave the car keys at the lot and keep the house keys safe at home. It completely eliminates the anxiety of leaning over a railing on a tour and hearing a distant clink.


10. Choose your footwear strategically. For our Italy trip, I only packed one pair of casual sandals and one 'dress up' pair. It was a mistake not to bring a second walking shoe, simply because my feet ache if I don't rotate my footwear. I ended up buying Birkenstocks in Rome just to give my feet a break—though, for the record, they are so much cheaper in Italian retail stores than in the US!

On the flip side, when we went to Japan, I overcompensated by packing extra shoes for my husband and the kids in case theirs got wet. Total waste of space. Since I had already waterproofed their primary shoes, and they're used to wearing the same pair every day anyway, the extras just took up valuable luggage real estate.

11. Embrace the vacation laundry day. For both our Italy and Japan trips, I intentionally booked an Airbnb with a washing machine right at the halfway mark. To bridge the gaps, I packed TSA-approved laundry soap strips, which are a lifesaver for washing socks and underwear in a hotel sink. Pair those with a universal flat silicone oap strips, and you never have to worry about a missing hotel plug. This exact setup saved the day in Venice when one of the kids dropped gelato all over his dinner shirt, allowing us to run a quick sink emergency wash instead of carrying a stained shirt for a week.

Ditching the 'just in case' mindset takes a little bit of bravery, but the payoff is instant freedom the second you step off the plane. Skip the baggage carousel, bypass the heavy lifting, and see how much lighter your trip feels when you're living out of a backpack. Your back—and your family—will thank you. Bon voyage!
